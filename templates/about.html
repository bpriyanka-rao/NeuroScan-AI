  What is Alzheimer's?

Alzheimer's disease is a progressive neurological disorder that causes the brain to shrink (atrophy) and brain cells to die. It is the most common cause of dementia, a continuous decline in thinking, behavioral and social skills that affects a person's ability to function independently.

The disease is characterized by the accumulation of amyloid plaques and tau tangles in brain tissue, which disrupts communication between neurons and eventually leads to their death. The hippocampus — responsible for forming new memories — is typically one of the first regions affected.

Genetic: APOE ε4 gene is the strongest risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's
Age: Risk doubles every 5 years after age 65
Cardiovascular: High BP, diabetes, and obesity increase risk
Lifestyle: Physical inactivity and social isolation are modifiable risk factors
Education: Higher cognitive reserve may delay symptom onset

  Why Early Detection Matters

AI Changes the Equation

Earlier diagnosis means earlier access to treatments that may slow progression
Lifestyle interventions are most effective in pre-symptomatic and early stages
Gives families more time to plan care, legal, and financial arrangements
Clinical trial eligibility is highest in early disease stages
AI-assisted MRI analysis can detect changes up to 10 years before symptoms appear
